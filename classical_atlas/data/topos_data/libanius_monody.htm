Libanius, Monody on the Temple of Apollo at Daphne

§ 1  Monody on the Temple of Apollo at Daphne
Fellow-citizens, whose eyes like mine are now involved in darkness, this city we shall no longer style beautiful or great.

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 2  . . A king of Persia, one of the ancestors of him who is now at war with us, having by treachery taken and burnt the city, as he was preparing the same fate for Daphne, was so thoroughly diverted from his purpose by the deity that, throwing away the torch which he brandished, he prostrated himself and adored Apollo: so appeased was his resentment, so checked was his fury

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 3  He, though he led an army against us, thought proper to preserve this temple, and the beauty of the image restrained his barbaric fury. But now, O heaven and earth, who and whence is that traitor, who wanting neither hoplites nor horse nor light forces, has consumed the whole with a small spark?

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 4  Nor was our temple destroyed by a violent storm, but in a serene and cloudless sky.

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 5  Hitherto, Apollo, though your altars were going thirsty for blood, you have remained the constant and careful guardian of Daphne; and though neglected and so far despised as to be stripped of your outward ornaments, you aquiesced. But now, after many sheep, many oxen, have been offered and you have received a King's kiss on your foot; seeing him whom you foretold, seen by him whom you revealed, and delivered from the wicked propinquity of a certain dead body whose nearness disturbed you, you have withdrawn from the midst of your worship. How can we now expect to be honoured, in future, by those who have a veneration for holy places and images!

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 6  When fatigued in our mind, of what a relief, O Zeus, are we deprived! How pure, how free from all tumults, was the locality of Daphne! how much still purer was the temple! like a harbor formed by nature within a harbor; both being tranquil, but the inner affording the most tranquility. Who did not there lose his diseases, his fears, his sorrows? Who there wished for the Isles of the Blessed?

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 7  Ere long will be the Olympic games; that annual festival will convene the cities; these cities too will come, bringing oxen as victims to Apollo. What then shall we do? Where shall we hide ourselves? Which of the gods will open the earth for us? What herald, what trumpet, will excite anything but tears? Who now will style the Olympic games a festival, as this late misfortune suggests so dire a lamentation?

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 8  Bring me my bow of horn,
says the tragedy.
I add, a little in the spirit of the prophecy,
That thus I may attack, and thus destroy,
The vile incendiary.
O impious deed! O sacrilegious soul! O daring hand! Surely this was another Tityus or Idas, the brother of Lynceus, not an archer, indeed, like the one or a giant like the other, but one proficient in nothing except frenzy against the gods. The sons of Aloeus, while they meditated mischief against the gods, you, Apollo, quieted by death; but him, bringing far from afar, your arrow did not arrest, transfixing his heart.

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 9  O wicked hand of Telchin!
O injurious fire! What did it first catch? Where did the evil begin? Seizing the roof, did it descend to the interior parts, to the head, the face, the cup, the tiara, or the flowing robe? Hephaistos, the treasurer of fire, though indebted to the god for his former obliging discovery, did not rebuke this wasting flame. Nor did Zeus, who has the command of rain, pour water on it, though for the unfortunate king of Lydia he extinguished the funeral pile.

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 10  What was the first suggestion of him who undertook this enterprise? whence this rashness? how could he restrain his fury? How could he avoid abandoning his purpose through reverence for the beauty of the god?

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 11  I turn my thought, countrymen, to the likeness of the god, and reason sets before my eyes his image, the gentleness of his aspect, the softness of the skin expressed in the stone, the belt over his breast confining the golden chiton, so that some parts of it subsided and others rose. What mind had such fervour that the whole appearance of the statue could not calm? For the god seemed in the act of singing; or as when he was once heard playing on his harp at noon. The song was in praise of the Earth, on whom, gaping to receive the virgin, and then contracting to conceal her, he seemed to pour a libation from a golden cup.

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 12  At the eruption of the flames the traveller exclaimed, the dear resident of Daphne, the priestess of the god, was alarmed; the beating of bosoms and shrill shrieks, echoing through the many-treed locality, soon reached the city, diffusing universal grief and horror. The ruler, whose eye had scarce yielded to sleep, at the dreadful news sprung from his bed. Transported with fury, and wishing for the wings of Hermes, he rushed forth to investigate the cause. Inwardly he burnt no less than the temple. The rafters now fell, scattering the fire below, which destroyed all that was within its reach: the Apollo immediately, being near to the roof; then other ornaments of the temple, the muses, the statues of the founders, the splendid marbles, the beautiful pillars. Crowds of spectators stood by lamenting, but unable to assist, like those who from land beholding a shipwreck can afford no relief but their tears.

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 13  The nymphs, leaving their fountains, loudly exclaimed; so did Zeus who sat not far distant, lamenting, as became him, the tarnished honours of his son; so did also an innumerable throng of daemons who inhabit the grove. Nor less was the lamentations of Calliope, in the middle of the city, when the chorus-leader of the Muses was wronged by the flames . . .

Event Date: 362 GR

§ 14  As propitious may you now be to me, Apollo, as Chryses rendered you when he imprecated vengeance on the Greeks, full of indignation, and dark as night. Since while we were offering sacrifices to you, and were restoring whatever had been purloined from your temple, the object of our worship has been snatched away from us; like a bridegroom who, while the garlands are weaving for his nuptials, dies.
